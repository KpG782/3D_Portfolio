# Brainstorming

Generate a real spread of options, stress-test them, and converge on a
recommendation — instead of jumping to the first idea.

## When to use
Invoke when the user asks for ideas, concepts, directions, alternatives, or
"how could we do X". Especially before committing to a design or build.

## Method

### 1. Frame
State the objective in one line and list the hard constraints (audience,
brand, tech, performance, time). Ideas are judged against these, not taste
alone.

### 2. Diverge (quantity first)
Produce **at least 4–6 genuinely distinct** options — not variations of one.
Push for range: safe → bold, literal → lateral. For each, give:
- a short name,
- the core idea in 1–2 sentences,
- why it could work.
Do not self-censor here; weird ideas often sharpen the good one.

### 3. Pressure-test (converge)
Score each option against the constraints. For the top contenders, name:
- **Strengths** (what it nails),
- **Risks/cost** (effort, performance, accessibility, maintenance),
- **Fit** with the stated goal and brand.
Kill options that lose; say why.

### 4. Recommend
Pick ONE (or a small combo) and justify it in terms of the objective and
constraints. Offer a clear runner-up. Note what you'd validate next.

## Principles
- Distinct beats numerous-but-samey. 4 real directions > 10 tweaks.
- Tie every idea back to the user's goal and audience.
- Be honest about trade-offs; a recommendation without risks is a sales pitch.
- When the choice materially changes the work, surface it to the user (e.g.
  via AskUserQuestion) rather than guessing.

## Output shape
1. Objective + constraints
2. Options (named, with the why)
3. Trade-off comparison of the finalists
4. Recommendation + runner-up + next step to validate
